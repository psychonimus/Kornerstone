If not Test Automation, then what?

Automation today has become a buzzword across industries. From core industries like manufacturing to front ending industries like customer service, everybody is turning to automation to streamline processes, increase efficiency, and optimize costs. However, as the world struggles with the increasing environmental challenges, the sustainability of automation can’t be overlooked.

While automation offers undeniable benefits, focusing solely on it can lead to unintended consequences:

Increased Maintenance Burden: Automated tests require ongoing maintenance to ensure accuracy as the application evolves. This can become a time-consuming task, negating the initial efficiency gains.

Brittle Tests: Tests become brittle if they are overly reliant on specific UI elements or functionality. Minor changes in the application can lead to a cascade of failing tests, hindering progress.

Overlooking the Intangibles: Automation excels at repetitive tasks but struggles to capture user experience, usability, or edge-case scenarios. These crucial aspects require human expertise.

Reduced Testers' Skills: Overdependence on automation can lead to a decline in core testing skills. Testers may become reliant on scripts and lose the ability to think critically and adapt to unexpected situations.

When we talk about automation, it is important to consider it in entirety. While some forms of automation may lead to reduced carbon emissions and resource efficiency, others may exacerbate inequalities, displace workers, and contribute to environmental degradation.

So, if not sustainable automation, then what? Let's delve into alternative approaches to ensure quality assurance without compromising sustainability.

1. Strategic Automation

Rather than automating tests indiscriminately, teams should practice strategic automation. This involves carefully evaluating which tests are suitable for automation and which are better suited for manual testing. By prioritizing critical tests and avoiding unnecessary automation, teams can minimize resource consumption and reduce environmental impact.

2. Balanced Test Strategy

A balanced test strategy combines both automated and manual testing approaches to achieve optimal results. While automation can provide efficiency gains for repetitive and deterministic tests, manual testing remains essential for exploratory testing, usability testing, and edge case scenarios. By striking the right balance between automation and manual testing, teams can ensure thorough test coverage while conserving resources.

3. Adopting Reusable and Repurposed automation

Harnessing the power of reusability and adaptability can propel your testing practices into the future. Thoughtful contemplation before initiating the creation of new test assets, ensuring your repository remains meticulously organized and optimized for efficiency, lays the groundwork for seamless collaboration and innovation

Embrace the potential of test assets originally designed for QA environments, repurposing them seamlessly for automation in production environments, and vice versa. This symbiotic relationship between testing environments enhances flexibility and agility, enabling swift adaptation to evolving requirements and scenarios.

Furthermore, unlock unparalleled efficiency by deploying the same test assets across diverse business processes, eliminating redundant efforts and streamlining operations. By breaking down silos and promoting cross-functional collaboration, organizations can maximize resource utilization and drive transformative change.

4. Ethical Test Data Usage

Test automation often relies on large datasets for validation and verification purposes. However, the indiscriminate use of data raises concerns about privacy, consent, and data sovereignty. To address these issues, teams should adopt ethical guidelines for test data usage, ensuring compliance with data protection regulations and respecting user privacy rights. By prioritizing ethical considerations, teams can build trust with stakeholders and mitigate potential risks associated with data misuse.

5. Continuous Improvement

Sustainable test automation is an ongoing process that requires continuous evaluation and improvement. Teams should regularly review their testing practices, identify areas for optimization, and implement iterative changes to enhance sustainability. This may involve conducting regular retrospectives, gathering feedback from team members, and embracing a culture of continuous learning and adaptation.

6. Community Collaboration:

Collaboration within the testing community is essential for advancing sustainable practices. By sharing best practices, tools, and resources, testers can collectively work towards reducing environmental impact and promoting social responsibility in testing activities. Participating in industry forums, conferences, and online communities allows testers to exchange ideas, learn from each other's experiences, and drive positive change in the field of quality assurance.

In conclusion, Automation can be a big help, but it's not a magic solution for testing everything. By embracing a balanced approach that combines automation with skilled manual testing, we can create a robust and efficient testing ecosystem.
